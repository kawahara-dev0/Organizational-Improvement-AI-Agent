Section 1 — What this overview is for

This document gives non-confidential context about how the Organization is structured, what it does, and how teams fit together. It complements the Employee Handbook, policies, and periodic strategy memos. Numbers and examples are illustrative for a mid-sized software organization.

Section 2 — What we do

The Organization builds cloud software that helps other businesses automate operational workflows (approvals, handoffs, notifications, and light reporting). Customers are typically small and mid-sized organizations across several regions.

The product is sold on a subscription basis. Implementation is mostly self-serve with optional professional services for larger rollouts.

Section 3 — Scale and footprint

Team size: approximately 80–150 people, including full-time employees and long-term contractors in core roles.

Locations: distributed by design. There is no single mandatory headquarters day for all roles; some regions maintain small collaboration hubs for teams that choose to meet in person.

Markets: revenue is diversified across regions; go-to-market and support operate in multiple time zones.

Section 4 — Organization structure (high level)

Work is grouped into the following functions. Titles and exact reporting lines may evolve; this section describes stable buckets for navigation and internal requests.

Product Engineering: product discovery, application engineering, quality practices, developer tooling, reliability.

Product Management & Design: roadmap choices, UX and research, pricing packaging input (with Finance).

Customer Success: onboarding, renewals, adoption metrics, escalation coordination with Engineering.

Go-to-Market (Marketing & Sales): demand generation, partnerships, sales cycles, brand.

Corporate (Finance, People, Legal, IT): payroll and benefits administration, hiring coordination, contracts, procurement, internal systems.

Executive leadership sets annual goals, approves major investments, and resolves cross-functional trade-offs. People Operations owns policy interpretation for workplace topics unless a policy names another owner.

Section 5 — How decisions are made (simplified)

Day-to-day product and technical decisions sit with product triads (engineering, product, design) within agreed guardrails (security, availability, cost).

Customer-impacting changes follow a release and communication process published by Engineering leadership.

People and workplace policy changes are sponsored by People Operations with executive sign-off for material updates.

Individual career and performance decisions are owned by managers with calibration support described in the Compensation & Benefits Policy.

Section 6 — Values (illustrative)

These statements guide internal communication; they do not replace policies.

Customer trust first: ship work that is explainable, observable, and recoverable when things go wrong.

Respectful candor: disagree in the open with professionalism; escalate when stuck.

Sustainable pace: prefer predictable delivery over heroics that burn people out.
